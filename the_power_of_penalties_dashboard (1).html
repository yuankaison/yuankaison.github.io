The Power of Penalties: Negativity-Aware Incentives for High-Quality Crowdsourced Data Labeling

Authors: Kai Wang, Runze Wu*, Yu Xiong, Haifeng Sun, Anran Li, Shaojie Tang, Changjie Fan, Xiang-Yang Li*
Affiliations: USTC, NetEase Fuxi AI Lab, University at Buffalo

Abstract

High-quality data labeling is essential for training robust ML models; however, existing reward allocation methods ignore fraud or assume non-negative worker utility, failing to penalize harmful contributions. To address this, we propose the Negativity-Aware Incentive (NAI) mechanism. It introduces two components: First, the Ability-Result Characteristic Function (AR-CF) adapts Shapley value theory through signed valuation to capture both positive and negative contributions. Second, a dynamic stake pool mechanism employs pre-commitment economics to balance fairness and operational efficiency. Deployed on NetEase Youling (430,000 registered workers), NAI achieves 16.6% accuracy improvement on video tasks and up to 33.9% fraud reduction.

01. Game-Theoretic Framework & Math

How NAI defines contribution using Signed Valuations and Dynamic Parameters.

v(S)

Ability-Result CF (AR-CF)

Models both worker abilities and raw correct/incorrect decisions. Rather than assuming non-negative inputs, the characteristic value maps to polarized domains.

v(S) = \frac{1}{|S|} ∑i ∈ S (ziqi - (1-zi)qi)
Where zi ∈ {0,1} represents correctness, and qi ∈ (0,1) is worker ability.
φi

Signed Shapley Valuation

Evaluates the marginal contribution of worker i across all possible cooperative coalitions S. If adding a worker degrades coalition correctness, they score a negative marginal share.

φi(v) = ∑ \frac{|S|!(n - |S| - 1)!}{n!} [v(S∪{i}) - v(S)]
Symmetrical reward-penalty: rewards expertise while penalizing sloppy, low-quality, or fraudulent actions.
Si

Dynamic Stake Pool

Pre-commitment economics blocks malicious actors. Workers post stakes before receiving tasks. Low-ability workers post larger collateral, creating strong quality-risk incentives.

Si = T · α · p (1 - qi)
Where T is expected tasks, α is the penalty factor, and p is standard task cost.
